Official Scoring Formula
Exact formula from the rules manual

📊 Formula

Final Score (FS) = Assessment Score (TAS) + Distance Score (TDS)

TAS = Team Assessment Score (your test score ÷ highest test score)
TDS = Distance Score = CDS ÷ Team Distance
CDS = Competition Distance Score = the closest distance to target point among all teams
Max score = 100 points
TermMeaning
CDSCompetition Distance Score — the closest distance from target among ALL teams (smallest number wins)
CASCompetition Assessment Score — highest written test score
TDSTeam Distance Score — your vehicle's distance from target point in cm
TASTeam Assessment Score — your written test score

★ Worked Example

Best team stops 0.5 cm from target (CDS=0.5). Your team stops 25 cm away. TDS = 0.5÷25 = 0.02. Best assessment = 13, your score = 6. TAS = 6÷13 = 0.46. FS = 0.46 + 0.02 = 0.48. The closer your vehicle, the higher your Distance Score.

Study Guide — Simple Machines & Physics
Know these topics for the written assessment

The Six Simple Machines

Simple machines make work easier by changing the force or distance required. Know all six types — their definition, how they work, and real-world examples.

1. Lever

  • 1st class: Fulcrum between effort and load (seesaw, scissors)
  • 2nd class: Load between fulcrum and effort (wheelbarrow)
  • 3rd class: Effort between fulcrum and load (tweezers, arm)
  • Mechanical advantage = load arm ÷ effort arm

2. Pulley

  • Fixed pulley: Changes direction of force only (flagpole)
  • Movable pulley: Reduces force needed (crane)
  • Block and tackle: Multiple pulleys, great mechanical advantage
  • More pulleys = more mechanical advantage

3. Wheel & Axle

  • A wheel attached to a smaller axle — they rotate together
  • Force applied to wheel is multiplied at the axle
  • Examples: doorknob, steering wheel, K'nex wheels
  • MA = radius of wheel ÷ radius of axle

4. Inclined Plane

  • A sloped surface that reduces force needed to lift
  • Trades reduced force for greater distance
  • Examples: ramp, stairs, skateboard ramp, wheelchair access
  • The gravity car ramp is an inclined plane!

5. Wedge

  • Two inclined planes joined back-to-back
  • Converts downward force into sideways force to split or separate
  • Examples: knife blade, axe head, doorstop, chisel

6. Screw

  • An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder
  • Converts rotational motion into forward movement
  • Examples: jar lid, wood screw, bolt, spiral staircase
  • More threads per cm = greater mechanical advantage
Gravity & Friction on Wheeled Vehicles

⬇ Gravity

  • Pulls all objects toward Earth's center
  • Accelerates vehicles down the ramp (converts GPE → KE)
  • The higher the ramp start line, the more potential energy — and more speed at the bottom
  • On a flat track, gravity no longer accelerates — friction slows the vehicle
  • Formula: GPE = mgh (mass × gravity × height)

🛞 Friction

  • Rolling friction: Between wheels and track surface — much lower than sliding
  • Sliding friction: When wheels lock up — much higher, causes skids
  • Friction is what stops the vehicle after leaving the ramp
  • More friction = vehicle stops sooner (useful for precise distance control)
  • Smoother surfaces have lower friction coefficients

Build Strategy
Recommended approach for the 25-minute session
1

Assign roles immediately

Minutes 0–1

One student starts the written test. The other leads the K'nex build. Agree on your starting ramp line before the clock starts — higher start = more speed but harder to stop at exactly 5.0 m.

2

Build the chassis first

Minutes 1–8

Build a stable rectangular or flat frame with 4 wheels. Make sure axles are straight and wheels spin freely — friction at the axle wastes energy and makes distance unpredictable.

  • All 4 wheels must touch the ground evenly
  • Rear wheels should be the main drive wheels
  • Attach the front fixed K'nex piece 1–2 cm above track level
3

Create a golf ball holder

Minutes 8–12

The golf ball must stay in the vehicle through the entire run. Build a cradle or bumper that securely holds the ball. It must not fall out — that is a Tier 2 violation.

4

Practice runs (up to 3)

Minutes 12–22

With permission from the Event Supervisor, make up to 3 test runs. For each run record: which start line, where the vehicle stopped. Use this data to choose your official run position.

  • Aim to land within 5 cm of the target each practice run
  • If overshooting: try a lower ramp start line or wider wheels (more friction)
  • If undershooting: try a higher ramp start or check wheel alignment
5

Finalize and lock in your position

Minutes 22–25

Choose your ramp start line based on practice data. Notify the Event Supervisor when ready. Give your estimate of how close you will get to the target point as part of the written test.

Competition Tips
What separates good runs from great ones
⚖️

Wheel alignment is everything

Crooked wheels create drag that's impossible to predict run-to-run. Spend time at the start making sure all four wheels spin freely and the axles are parallel.

📊

Track your data

Write down exactly where your vehicle stopped for each practice run relative to the target. If run 1 = +15 cm over, move to a lower start line. Systematic data beats guessing.

🏁

Consistent release matters

Use the same 2-hand release every time. Small differences in how you let go can change where the vehicle stops by several centimeters. Practice being consistent.

⛳

Secure the golf ball first

The ball must stay in the vehicle through the run. Build the holder before doing any test runs — a Tier 2 violation wipes 500 points from your score.

📏

Use multiple ramp start lines

The ramp has start lines at 20 cm intervals. Use your practice runs to figure out exactly which line gives you the closest stop to 5.0 m.

✍️

Estimate accurately — it's a tiebreaker

Your estimated distance from the target is Tiebreaker 1. Based on your practice runs, give a specific, realistic estimate — not "as close as possible." A precise estimate wins ties.

📝

Study the six simple machines

Assessment is 50% of your score. Know all six simple machines, their classes (especially levers), and how gravity and friction interact with your vehicle. Start studying well before competition day.

🔩

K'nex stability beats complexity

A simple, sturdy 4-wheel chassis that rolls straight beats an elaborate design that wobbles. Don't over-engineer — focus on straight wheels and a secure golf ball holder.
