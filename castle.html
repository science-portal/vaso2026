Taken concurrently during the 30-minute build. Topics: the history of catapults, types of catapults (Ballista, Onager, Trebuchet, Mangonel), and how they work. Teams also estimate their distance to the target in centimeters.

Official Scoring Formula
Exact formula from the rules manual

📊 Formula

Final Score (FS) = Assessment Score (TAS) + Distance Score (TDS)

TAS = (Team Assessment Score ÷ Highest Assessment Score)
TDS = (Competition Distance Score ÷ Team Distance Score)
CDS = closest distance to target center among all teams
Max score = 100 points

★ Worked Example

Best team lands 0.5 cm from target center (CDS=0.5). Your team lands 25 cm away. TDS = 0.5÷25 = 0.02 pts. Best assessment = 13, yours = 6. TAS = 6÷13 = 0.46 pts. FS = 0.46 + 0.02 = 0.48. Closer = better distance score.

Tiebreakers

  • Tiebreaker 1: Closest to the center of the target
  • Tiebreaker 2: Answers to select questions on the written test
Study Guide — Catapults
History, types, and mechanics — know all for the written test

History of Catapults

  • First developed in ancient Mesopotamia and Egypt as simple stone-throwing devices
  • Greeks and Romans greatly improved catapult technology — more accuracy and power
  • Used primarily in siege warfare to breach walls and fortifications
  • Also used in field battles to disrupt enemy formations
  • Led to major advances in engineering and mechanics
  • Medieval catapults could hurl projectiles weighing hundreds of pounds
Types of Catapults

🎯 Ballista

  • Resembles a giant crossbow
  • Uses torsion (twisted rope or sinew) for power
  • Launches heavy bolts (large arrows) in a flat trajectory
  • Very accurate compared to other types
  • Used to target soldiers and fortification defenders

🪨 Onager

  • Simple single-arm catapult using torsion power
  • The arm is pulled back against a twisted rope bundle
  • When released, the arm swings up and hurls the projectile
  • Named after the wild donkey (kicks like one)
  • Could hurl large stones long distances

⚔️ Trebuchet

  • Uses a counterweight swinging arm — not torsion
  • Very large and powerful — could launch boulders
  • Most accurate of the large catapults
  • Required a large crew to operate
  • Used heavily in medieval castle sieges

🔥 Mangonel

  • Torsion-powered, similar to the Ballista but single-arm
  • Curved spoon-shaped arm holds the projectile
  • Can launch a variety of items: stones, fire pots, debris
  • Simpler to build than a trebuchet
  • Less accurate but quick to reload
Physics of Projectile Motion

📐 Launch Angle

  • 45° angle gives the maximum horizontal range on flat ground
  • Lower angle = flatter trajectory, shorter range
  • Higher angle = more height, shorter range
  • Adjust arm tension and position to change angle

⚡ Elastic Potential Energy

  • Rubber bands store energy when stretched
  • When released, EPE converts to kinetic energy of the arm
  • More stretch = more energy = farther launch
  • Stiffer bands (#19) store more energy per unit stretch

Build Strategy
30 minutes to build and test your catapult
1

Choose your catapult design

Minutes 0–3

Two viable designs with these materials: (1) Spoon catapult — use the box as a base, attach one bamboo spoon as a throwing arm anchored with rubber bands. (2) Double-spoon trebuchet — tape spoons together for a longer arm. Option 1 is faster to build; option 2 can be more consistent.

2

Build the base

Minutes 3–8

Score and fold the mailing box into a stable L-shape or rectangular base. Stability is critical — if the catapult tips over during launch, distance is unpredictable.

  • Use tape to reinforce corners and edges
  • Set it on the table and push it — it should not slide or tip
3

Attach the throwing arm

Minutes 8–15

Tape or rubber-band a spoon so it can rotate and snap forward. Attach the rubber band(s) to the arm to power it — more bands or stiffer bands (#19) = more launch force.

  • The "bowl" of the spoon holds the Dots candy projectile
  • Arm should swing freely without binding on the box sides
4

Test runs + calibration

Minutes 15–26

With supervisor permission, make test launches. Measure each one against the 150 cm target. Adjust: rubber band stretch (pull arm back more/less), launch angle, or which rubber band size you use.

  • Overshooting: use a shorter pull-back, fewer/smaller bands
  • Undershooting: use a longer pull-back, more/larger bands
5

Lock in and estimate

Minutes 26–30

Mark your launch position with a piece of tape. Record your estimate of the distance to the target (this is part of the written test and Tiebreaker 1). Aim to give a specific number in centimeters based on your test runs.

Competition Tips
How to get your Dots candy to land on target
📍

Consistency over power

The goal is 150 cm exactly — not as far as possible. A catapult that launches 149 cm every single time beats one that averages 150 but varies by ±30 cm.

📏

Mark your draw position

Once you find a pull-back that hits 150 cm, use a small tape mark to repeat that exact position for your official launch. Inconsistent draw length = inconsistent distance.

🟡

Choose rubber bands strategically

#19 bands are short and stiff — good for shorter draws with high power. #64 bands are long and looser. Try different combinations in your test runs to find what gives 150 cm consistently.

🏗️

Stable base wins

If your catapult slides or tips during the launch, your distance will be completely different every time. Weight the base with extra cardboard or use most of your tape keeping the base down and solid.

🎯

Aim for the center line

Distance to target matters, but landing on the center line helps with Tiebreaker 1. Adjust your catapult angle left/right as well as distance.

📚

Study all four catapult types

Know Trebuchet vs. Ballista vs. Onager vs. Mangonel cold. Understand which uses counterweight (Trebuchet) vs. torsion (Ballista, Onager, Mangonel). These distinctions are very likely to appear on the test.

✍️

Give a specific estimate

Your estimate of the landing distance is Tiebreaker 1. Don't say "about 150 cm" — say "147 cm" based on your test run data. A precise estimate wins ties.

🥄

Both spoons = longer arm

Taping both bamboo spoons end-to-end creates a longer throwing arm. A longer arm moves the projectile faster at release — useful if your catapult isn't reaching 150 cm with one spoon.
