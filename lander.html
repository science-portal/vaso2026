🚀 Astronaut Lander

Build a landing device that keeps a marshmallow astronaut safely inside its paper cup capsule after dropping from a two-story stairwell. Maximize flight time.

Grades 3–4 45-minute session 2 students per team
Event Overview
Official description from the VA Division A Rules Manual

📋 The Challenge

Build a landing device to safely protect a simulated astronaut (a regular marshmallow inside the provided paper cup capsule) from injury when landing from a two-story stairwell. Success = marshmallow stays in the cup. Score = flight time + written assessment (max 100 points).

⚠️ Safety — Required to Compete

  • ANSI Z87+ impact-rated safety glasses must be worn during any competitor's flight phase
  • ALL competitors wear eye protection — not just the team currently dropping
  • Must be stamped "Z87+" on the lens — eyeglasses are not safety rated
  • Teams must bring their own — not provided at event
  • No proper eye protection = no-show score

🚫 Key Restrictions

  • The 237 ml paper cup is mandatory — must be used, cannot be cut
  • Marshmallow may NOT be taped or wedged into the cup
  • Nothing may be built within 10 cm directly above the cup lip
  • All other materials may be cut except the marshmallows and the paper cup

📝 Written Assessment (simultaneous with build)

  • Taken SIMULTANEOUSLY during the 25-minute build phase
  • Topics: forces and factors experienced by an astronaut when landing back on Earth after a stay in space
  • Content includes: gravity, air resistance, impact force, g-force, microgravity effects on human body (bone density, muscle atrophy, fluid shift, cardiovascular changes)

Official Scoring Formula
Exact formula from the rules manual

📊 Formula

FS = (TAS ÷ CAS) × 50 + (TTS ÷ CFS) × 50 • Maximum = 100 points

★ Worked Example

CAS = 15, team scores 9 → TAS = (9÷15)×50 = 30 pts. CFS = 3.56 sec, team time = 1.45 sec → TTS = (1.45÷3.56)×50 = 20.4 pts. Final Score = 50.4 points.

Build Strategy
Recommended approach for the 45-minute session
1

Assign roles immediately

Minutes 0–2

One student leads the build; one takes the written test. Both must know the design plan before the clock starts.

  • Build lead: design around the mandatory coffee filter parachute first
  • Test taker: know gravity, drag, impact force, g-force, and microgravity effects
2

Design your parachute system

Minutes 2–12

The #6 coffee filter is larger — use it as your primary parachute. Attach 4+ equally-spaced string lines to the edge. Lines must be EQUAL length — unequal lines cause tilting and reduced drag.

  • Tie string to filter edge at 4 evenly-spaced points
  • Lines ~25 cm long — test that cup hangs level and upright when suspended
  • Respect the 10 cm rule: nothing may be built within 10 cm above cup lip
3

Build the structural frame

Minutes 12–22

Use straws, index cards, and cardboard to build a frame that holds the cup at the correct position below the parachute. Keep it lightweight.

  • Lighter = slower fall = more flight time
  • Frame must keep cup opening facing UP throughout descent
  • Use rubber bands to suspend cup from the frame — adds shock absorption
4

Connect parachute to frame

Minutes 22–30

Attach parachute strings to the SIDES of the cup or to the frame structure. Nothing crosses the 10 cm zone above the cup lip.

  • Test by holding your lander by the parachute — is the cup upright?
  • Tape all string attachment points — this joint takes full opening shock
5

Test drop and refine

Minutes 30–36

Do a gentle test drop from arm height (not full height). Check: Does the parachute open? Does the cup stay upright? Does the marshmallow stay in?

6

Announce estimate + final checks

Minutes 36–45

State your estimated flight time to the Event Supervisor. Load the marshmallow for the actual drop. Check all joints one final time.

  • Time your test drop with a stopwatch — give a real estimate, not a guess
  • Estimate accuracy is Tiebreaker 1 — it matters when scores are tied
Competition Tips
What wins — and what to avoid
☕

Use the #6 coffee filter

The #6 is larger than the #2 — more surface area means more drag, which means slower descent and longer flight time. Use it as your primary parachute every time.

⬆️

Keep the cup opening upright

The cup must face up throughout the entire descent or the marshmallow will fall out before landing. Test this: hold lander by the parachute strings and check the cup angle hangs level.

📐

The 10 cm rule is critical

Nothing may be built within 10 cm directly above the cup lip. Attach parachute strings to the SIDES of the cup or to a frame below — not crossing over the top opening.

⚖️

Go lighter to stay up longer

Every extra gram increases fall speed. Use minimum materials. The coffee filters are already very lightweight — don't add unnecessary weight from cardboard or straws.

⏱️

Time your drop and estimate accurately

Tiebreaker 1 is the team with the closest estimate to actual flight time. Use a stopwatch during practice. Give a specific number — not a round guess.

📚

Study both assessment topics

Assessment = 50 pts. Know gravity, air resistance, impact force, g-force, and what happens to bones, muscles, and the heart in space. Review before competition day.
