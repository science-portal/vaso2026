Official Scoring Formula
Exact formula from the rules manual

📊 Formula

Team Score = 0.5 × (THS ÷ CHS) + 0.5 × (TAS ÷ CAS) × [multipliers]

THS = Team Height Score (your bridge center height in mm)
CHS = Competition Height Score (tallest bridge among all teams)
TAS = Team Assessment Score (your written test score)
CAS = Competition Assessment Score (highest written test score)

★ Worked Example

Your bridge height = 320mm, tallest = 400mm → height ratio = 0.80. Your test = 8/10, best = 10 → assessment ratio = 0.80. Base score = 0.5×0.80 + 0.5×0.80 = 0.80. With 500g bonus: 0.5×(0.80×1.2) + 0.5×0.80 = 0.48 + 0.40 = 0.88

Study Guide — Truss Bridges
Know these topics for the written assessment

What Is a Truss Bridge?

A truss bridge uses a framework of interconnected triangles to carry loads. The triangular geometry makes it rigid and distributes forces efficiently. Each member carries either tension (pulling apart) or compression (pushing together) — never bending, in an ideal truss.

Types of Truss Bridges

Know which way the diagonals lean — this is the most-tested distinction.

Diagonals in TENSION
Pratt Truss

Diagonals slope toward the center. Verticals in compression, diagonals in tension. Most common design — very efficient for medium spans.

Diagonals in COMPRESSION
Howe Truss

Opposite of Pratt — diagonals slope away from center. Verticals in tension, diagonals in compression. Historically used with iron diagonals.

Alternating
Warren Truss

Alternating diagonal members with no verticals (basic form). Forms equilateral triangles. Simple and lightweight — a common competition choice.

Curved top chord
Parker Truss

A Pratt variant with a curved (polygonal) top chord, making the bridge deeper at center where bending moments are greatest.

Arch in compression
Bowstring Truss

Arched top chord acts like a bow under compression. Straight bottom chord acts as the bowstring in tension. Elegant and efficient.

Complex
K-Truss

Named for K-shaped panels with two diagonals meeting each vertical at mid-height. Used for tall, long-span bridges. Recognizable K pattern.

Road through truss
Through Truss

Traffic passes between the top and bottom chords. Provides overhead structure above the roadway. Common in older railroad bridges.

Road on top
Deck Truss

Traffic travels on top of the truss. Maximum clearance below. Opposite of a through truss. Used where underclearance matters.

Forces in Truss Members

TENSION — pulling apart

  • Forces pull the member apart — it stretches
  • Bottom chord of a loaded bridge = tension
  • Pratt truss diagonals = tension
  • Straw failures in tension happen at joints — tape carefully
  • Tension members can be thinner (straws are fine)

COMPRESSION — pushing together

  • Forces push the member together — it buckles
  • Top chord of a loaded bridge = compression
  • Howe truss diagonals = compression
  • Straw failures in compression = buckling (bending sideways)
  • Bundle 2–3 straws or slide one inside another to resist

Build Strategy
Recommended approach for the 25-minute build + test session
1

Assign roles immediately

Minutes 0–1

One student takes the written test solo. One student leads the build. Both must know the design plan, the 10 cm width rule, and the loading block clearance requirement before the clock starts.

2

Sketch and count straws

Minutes 1–4

Decide your truss type (Warren is beginner-friendly; Pratt maximizes height efficiency). Allocate: ~4 bottom chord, ~4 top chord, ~8 verticals/diagonals per side, rest for reinforcement and reserve.

  • Target height: as tall as possible while keeping width ≥10 cm
  • Unused straws = Tiebreaker 1 — plan to use nearly all of them
3

Build the first truss face flat

Minutes 4–12

Lay straws on a flat surface to form one side of the truss. Tape all joints before lifting. A flat build is faster and more precise than building in the air.

  • Top chord: reinforce by sliding one straw inside another for compression resistance
  • Tape joints from multiple angles — most failures happen at connections
4

Build second face + add width

Minutes 12–18

Mirror the first side. Connect both sides with cross-members at top and bottom to create a 3D bridge. Width must be ≥10.0 cm outside edge to outside edge.

  • Measure width now — easier to fix before it's fully assembled
  • X-pattern cross-bracing on the faces resists twisting (racking)
5

Verify loading block path

Minutes 18–22

The 5×5 cm loading block must be able to pass through the entire length of the bridge in any orientation. Check this explicitly — violations cost a 0.5× height penalty.

6

Measure height + final reinforcement

Minutes 22–25

Check center height. Reinforce any loose joints. Add final cross-bracing with remaining straws. Aim to use all or nearly all straws — unused straws are Tiebreaker 1.

Competition Tips
What wins bridge building competitions
🔺

Height is 50% of your score

Build as tall as possible. A 400mm bridge scores 33% better than a 300mm bridge on the height component alone. Every millimeter counts — don't stop early.

📏

Measure width twice

Width <10 cm at center = 0.5× penalty on your entire height score. Measure before and after connecting both sides. It's the most expensive mistake to make.

🔗

Reinforce compression members

The top chord carries compression — it's the most likely part to buckle. Slide one straw inside another, or bundle 2–3 straws, to dramatically increase buckling resistance.

🩹

Wrap tape at every joint

Wrap tape around each joint rather than just sticking it on the surface. A compression band around a connection is far stronger. Most failures happen at joints, not member mid-spans.

📦

Check the loading block path

The 5×5 cm loading block must pass fully through your bridge. Add internal cross-members only outside the loading block's path — don't block it or you lose 50% of height score.

⚖️

Go for the 500g bonus

A ×1.2 multiplier on your height score is significant. If your bridge held 300g with room to spare, it likely can hold 500g. Design for the bonus — it can be the difference between 1st and 3rd.

🥤

Save straws for tiebreakers

Unused complete straws win Tiebreaker 1. Don't cut straws unless necessary. Plan your design so you use nearly all straws but keep 2–3 whole ones if possible.

📝

Assessment is 50% too

Height and assessment each contribute equally. Know Pratt vs. Howe vs. Warren diagonals cold. Know tension vs. compression. Know the Method of Joints formula. Study before competition day.
