01 Event Overview
⚠ Safety Requirement Safety glasses labeled ANSI Z87+ (impact rated) are MANDATORY. Regular eyeglasses do NOT qualify. Teams without eye protection may be disqualified. All competitors must wear protection during the entire competition.
📋 What to Bring
  • ANSI Z87+ impact safety glasses (one per participant)
  • Pencil, pen, or marker
  • Ruler or straight edge
  • One pair of scissors per team member
🏗 Materials Provided at Event
  • 30 paper drinking straws (~20 cm each)
  • 1 meter of 2.54 cm tape
  • 300-gram and 500-gram test masses
  • 2 support structures (~5 × 9.5 × 30.5 cm)
  • 1 loading block (5 × 5 × 0.6 cm, 20 g)
02 Construction Phase — 25 Minutes
🔧 Building Rules
  • Build one truss bridge to span a 30.0 cm gap between the two support structures.
  • The bridge must hold a 300-gram or 500-gram mass placed on the loading block at the top and center point.
  • Bridge must be constructed entirely from the provided straws and tape. Straws may be cut or crimped to slide inside each other.
  • The bridge must have a width of at least 10.0 cm at its center point (outside edge to outside edge).
  • The bridge may not have internal cross members that would block the loading block from passing through its entire length in any orientation.
  • The bridge must support at least one of the two masses for 30 seconds to earn a competition score.
  • Using tools or materials not listed above results in placement below all other teams.
📝 Concurrent Written Assessment Both participants answer the written assessment questions during the 25-minute construction phase. The assessment covers types of bridges, building techniques, and forces within bridges.
03 Competition Phase
⚖ Testing Procedure
  • The Event Supervisor places supports parallel, 30.0 cm apart on a level flat surface.
  • Competitors set their bridge across the supports. No sticky tape may touch the top or sides of the supports.
  • ES measures and records: whether the bridge spans the gap, whether width ≥ 10.0 cm, and whether the loading block can pass through.
  • ES measures bridge height at the center to the nearest millimeter and counts unused, complete straws.
  • Competitors place the loading block on the top chord at the center, then place the 300-gram mass. No touching the bridge after the mass is placed.
  • Testing ends when the bridge fails, any part sags and touches the table, or 30 seconds elapses.
  • If the bridge holds 300 g for 30 seconds, testing is repeated with the 500-gram mass.

01 Introduction to Truss Bridges
What is a Truss Bridge?

A truss bridge is a bridge whose load-bearing structure is composed of a truss — a framework of interconnected triangles. The triangular geometry makes the structure rigid and distributes loads efficiently across the members. Truss bridges are one of the oldest and most popular types of bridges due to their strength-to-weight ratio.

✔ Advantages
  • High strength-to-weight ratio
  • Efficient use of materials
  • Can span long distances
  • Can be built from relatively small components
  • Triangular units distribute forces effectively
✘ Disadvantages
  • Complex to design and analyze
  • Requires more connections/joints
  • May be susceptible to vibrations (resonance)
  • Can be heavy compared to other modern designs
  • High maintenance cost for large-scale bridges
02 Basic Components
Top Chord

The upper horizontal member running the length of the bridge. In most truss designs, the top chord is in compression.

Bottom Chord

The lower horizontal member. In simple truss bridges, the bottom chord is typically in tension.

Verticals

Members that run perpendicular to the chords. Depending on the truss type, they can be in tension or compression.

Diagonals

Angled members that connect the top and bottom chords. Their force type (tension/compression) depends on the truss design.

Joints / Nodes

Connection points where truss members meet. Forces in a truss are assumed to act only at the joints.

Deck

The surface the load rests on. In this event, the loading block sits on the top chord at the center point.

03 Types of Truss Bridges

Know these bridge types for the written assessment — focus especially on diagonal orientation!

Pratt Truss

Vertical members are in compression; diagonal members are in tension. Diagonals slope toward the center. One of the most common designs.

Howe Truss

Opposite of Pratt — diagonal members are in compression, verticals in tension. Diagonals slope away from the center.

Warren Truss

Alternating diagonal members with no verticals (in the basic form). Diagonals alternate between tension and compression. Simple and efficient.

Parker Truss

A variation of the Pratt truss with a curved (polygonal) top chord, allowing the bridge to be deeper at the center where bending moments are greatest.

K-Truss

Named for the K-shaped panels. Each panel has a vertical and two diagonals meeting it at mid-height. Used for tall, long-span bridges.

Bowstring Truss

Arched top chord (like a bow) with a straight bottom chord acting as the bowstring in tension. The arch is in compression throughout.

Through Truss

The roadway passes through the truss (between the top and bottom chords). Examples: many historic railroad bridges.

Deck Truss

The roadway sits on top of the truss (on the top chord). Opposite of a through truss. Good for maximizing clearance below.

04 Forces in Truss Members
⬆ Tension

A member is in tension when forces pull it apart (stretching). Tension members tend to elongate. In straw bridges, tension members are at risk of pulling apart at joints — taping connections carefully is critical.

⬇ Compression

A member is in compression when forces push it together (squeezing). Compression members tend to buckle. In straw bridges, thin straws buckle under compression — doubling straws or cross-bracing helps.

📐 Method of Joints

Analyze forces at each joint by applying equilibrium equations (sum of horizontal forces = 0; sum of vertical forces = 0). Start at a joint with only two unknown forces. At each joint, the sum of all forces must balance. This method identifies whether each member is in tension (+) or compression (−).

✂ Method of Sections

Cut through the truss with an imaginary line intersecting no more than three members. Apply equilibrium to the free body of either side to solve for the forces in the cut members. Useful when only a few specific members need analysis.

05 Construction Techniques (Straw Bridge)
💡 Building Tips for This Event
  • Height = Score: Taller bridges score better. Maximize height while maintaining structural integrity.
  • Triangulate everything: Triangles are the most rigid 2D shape. All panels should form triangles.
  • Reinforce compression members: Bundle multiple straws together or slide one inside another to resist buckling.
  • Secure joints well: Tape joints firmly; most failures occur at connections, not in the members themselves.
  • Maintain 10.0 cm width: Measure carefully! Falling below 10 cm triggers a 0.5× height penalty.
  • Keep the path clear: No internal cross-members that would block the 5×5 cm loading block from passing through.
  • Plan before cutting: Sketch your design first. Efficient use of straws helps — unused straws are tiebreakers.
  • Work as a team: One builds, one answers assessment questions, then trade — or work in parallel on different sections.
