⚡ Basic Electricity

  • Electric current: Flow of electric charge (electrons) through a conductor. Measured in Amperes (A).
  • Voltage: Electrical potential difference that drives current. Measured in Volts (V).
  • Resistance: Opposition to current flow. Measured in Ohms (Ω).
  • Static electricity: Buildup of electric charge on an object's surface.
  • Conventional current: Flows + to − (opposite of actual electron flow).
  • Electron flow: Electrons flow from − to + terminal (negative to positive).

🔌 Ohm's Law

The fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance:

V = I × R
  • V = Voltage (Volts, V)
  • I = Current (Amperes, A)
  • R = Resistance (Ohms, Ω)
  • To find current: I = V ÷ R
  • To find resistance: R = V ÷ I
  • Example: 9V battery, 220Ω resistor → I = 9 ÷ 220 = 0.041 A (41 mA)

🔗 Series Circuits

  • Only ONE path for current to flow
  • If one component fails → entire circuit breaks (all go out)
  • Total resistance: R_total = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...
  • Current: Same throughout the entire circuit (I is constant)
  • Voltage: Splits across each component (voltages add up to total)
  • Example: Christmas lights that all go out when one breaks
  • The circuit you build in this event is a SERIES circuit

🔀 Parallel Circuits

  • MULTIPLE paths for current to flow
  • If one component fails → others continue working
  • Total resistance: Less than any individual resistor (1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2...)
  • Voltage: Same across each branch (V is constant)
  • Current: Splits between branches (currents add up to total)
  • Example: House outlets — each works independently

⚗️ Conductors & Insulators

  • Conductors: Allow electric current to flow easily
  • Examples: copper, silver, gold, aluminum, water, human body
  • Insulators: Resist the flow of electric current
  • Examples: rubber, plastic, glass, wood, air, ceramic
  • Semiconductors: Between conductors and insulators (e.g., silicon)
  • Insulators are used to coat wires for safety
  • The LED in your circuit is a semiconductor device

🔧 Breadboard & Circuit Build

  • Breadboard: Reusable board for prototyping circuits without soldering
  • Holes are connected in rows horizontally (in the middle sections)
  • LED: Light Emitting Diode — only works if current flows in correct direction (has polarity)
  • Long LED leg (+): Anode — connect to positive terminal
  • Short LED leg (−): Cathode — connect to negative terminal
  • Resistor (220Ω): Limits current to prevent burning out the LED
  • Circuit order: battery (+) → resistor → LED (+) → LED (−) → battery (−)

TermDefinition
Electric current (I)The flow of electric charge through a conductor. Measured in Amperes (A).
Voltage (V)Electrical potential difference between two points; the "push" that drives current. Measured in Volts (V).
Resistance (R)Opposition to the flow of electric current. Measured in Ohms (Ω).
Ohm's LawV = I × R — the fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance.
ConductorMaterial that allows electric current to flow easily (e.g., copper, water).
InsulatorMaterial that resists the flow of electric current (e.g., rubber, plastic, glass).
Static electricityBuildup of electric charge on the surface of an object that doesn't flow.
Electron flowActual movement of electrons from negative to positive terminal in a circuit.
Conventional currentDefined as flowing from positive to negative — opposite of electron flow.
Series circuitCircuit with only one path for current — total resistance is sum of all resistors.
Parallel circuitCircuit with multiple paths for current — total resistance is less than any single resistor.
LED (Light Emitting Diode)Semiconductor component that emits light when current flows through it in the correct direction.
ResistorComponent that limits current flow. A 220Ω resistor protects the LED from too much current.
BreadboardA reusable prototyping board for building circuits without soldering.
Anode (+)The positive terminal of an LED — the longer leg. Connect to the positive side of the circuit.
Cathode (−)The negative terminal of an LED — the shorter leg. Connect toward the negative terminal.
Direct current (DC)Electric current that flows in one constant direction (as from a battery).
